# Background, importance, novelty, and gap review

## Bottom line

The manuscript should not claim that it discovered multivariate selection, pollinator–herbivore interactions, defence-associated pollination costs, or ecological context dependence. Those foundations are established.

The defensible contribution is narrower:

> a focal-pair local diagnostic framework that separates antagonist relief, mutualist interference, and direct joint-cost curvature; states that the mechanism decomposition is not identified by total fitness alone; requires an explicit orientation gate before assigning non-negative mechanism labels; derives the full environmental derivative balance; and enforces strict boundaries between route existence, channel curvature, the complete mixed partial, trait covariance, and evolutionary endpoints.

A targeted review did not identify an earlier paper combining all of those elements for one floral attraction × flower-specific defence/barrier pair. This is a provisional positioning statement, not a claim of exhaustive priority.

## Established foundations

### 1. Multivariate and correlational selection are established

Lande and Arnold (1983) supplied the classical framework for measuring selection on correlated quantitative characters. Consequently, using a cross-trait fitness curvature or mixed term is not itself novel.

Reference: Lande R, Arnold SJ. 1983. The measurement of selection on correlated characters. *Evolution* 37:1210–1226. DOI: 10.2307/2408842.

### 2. Pollinators and herbivores can have non-additive effects on plant fitness

Herrera et al. (2002) experimentally showed that pollinator and herbivore effects on plant fitness can interact and proposed a route toward correlated evolution of mutualism- and antagonism-related traits. Therefore, the general statement that mutualists and antagonists jointly shape plant traits is not new.

Reference: Herrera CM et al. 2002. Interaction of pollinators and herbivores on plant fitness suggests a pathway for correlated evolution of mutualism- and antagonism-related traits. *PNAS* 99:16823–16828. DOI: 10.1073/pnas.252362799.

### 3. Attractive floral signals can recruit antagonists

Theis and Adler (2012) manipulated floral fragrance and found that increased fragrance attracted florivorous beetles and reduced plant reproduction. This supports an `A -> antagonist exposure` pathway but does not identify the complete attraction × defence mixed curvature.

Reference: Theis N, Adler LS. 2012. Advertising to the enemy: enhanced floral fragrance increases beetle attraction and reduces plant reproduction. *Ecology* 93:430–435. DOI: 10.1890/11-0825.1.

### 4. Antagonist-control benefits can alter selection on floral signals

Knauer, Bakhtiari, and Schiestl (2018) showed that crab spiders can indirectly affect floral-signal evolution by removing florivores. This demonstrates that the net role of an organism that can deter pollinators may change when antagonist suppression is included. It is a strong biological example of conditional net effects, but it is not the same as the present focal-trait sign criterion.

Reference: Knauer AC, Bakhtiari M, Schiestl FP. 2018. Crab spiders impact floral-signal evolution indirectly through removal of florivores. *Nature Communications* 9:1367. DOI: 10.1038/s41467-018-03792-x.

### 5. Mutualism–antagonism balance can switch across ecological regimes

Population-dynamic models have shown transitions between mutualistic and antagonistic outcomes when one interaction partner supplies benefits at one life stage and costs at another. Such work establishes that context-dependent sign changes are a broader ecological idea; the current manuscript should not claim novelty for regime transitions themselves.

Example: Revilla TA, Encinas-Viso F. 2014. Dynamical transitions in a pollination–herbivory interaction. arXiv:1404.4804.

## The unresolved problem

The literature establishes many relevant routes separately, but these results do not automatically answer the focal question:

> For one explicitly defined floral attraction trait `A`, one explicitly defined flower-specific defence/barrier trait `D`, and one declared outcome scale `W`, when does `D` increase versus decrease the local marginal return to `A`?

Four inferential gaps remain.

### Gap 1: net effects do not identify mechanisms

A measured total fitness surface can show `W_AD != 0`, but the same smooth cross term can be allocated differently among mutualist benefit, antagonist loss, and direct cost. Total `W` therefore does not uniquely identify antagonist relief, mutualist interference, or joint-cost curvature.

### Gap 2: trait labels do not imply cross-curvature signs

Calling `D` a defence trait does not prove that it changes the marginal antagonist cost of `A`. Likewise, showing that `D` reduces pollinator use does not prove that it reduces the marginal pollination return to the same focal `A`. The mechanism labels require an explicit derivative argument or channel-specific experiment.

### Gap 3: context dependence is often stated without a directional derivative law

Saying that outcomes are context dependent does not specify which ecological change moves the attraction–defence interaction toward complementarity or substitutability. The full derivative balance must allow all channel contributions to depend on both pollinator service and antagonist pressure.

### Gap 4: route evidence is frequently over-promoted

Evidence that one defence/barrier reduces pollinator use establishes a possible route. It does not estimate a focal mixed curvature, combine heterogeneous traits into one `D` axis, calibrate a regime map, predict trait covariance, or establish an evolutionary endpoint.

## Defensible novelty

The manuscript's novelty should be stated as an inference architecture, not as a new biological interaction or a new mathematical derivative.

### Core contribution

1. define one focal `A`, one focal `D`, and one outcome scale `W`;
2. separate the signed bookkeeping identity from biological mechanism identification;
3. require an orientation gate before using non-negative mechanism magnitudes;
4. express the local sign as antagonist relief minus mutualist interference minus direct joint-cost curvature;
5. derive unrestricted environmental derivatives before presenting separable and linear special cases;
6. distinguish a local marginal interaction from trait covariance, optimal trait combinations, and evolutionary dynamics;
7. treat literature records as route-level context unless the focal channel curvature is directly identified.

### Safe novelty sentence

> We develop a focal-trait local diagnostic framework that identifies the assumptions required to interpret attraction–defence fitness curvature mechanistically and specifies when ecological changes shift that interaction toward complementarity or substitutability.

### Sentence to avoid

> We provide the first model showing that pollinators and herbivores jointly determine floral evolution.

That claim is contradicted by established empirical and theoretical work.

## Why the paper matters

The paper addresses a recurring interpretive error in floral-evolution research: treating attraction and defence as inherently opposed categories, or interpreting any observed trait association as evidence of a fixed allocation trade-off.

Its importance is methodological and conceptual:

- the same defence/barrier can protect flowers from antagonists while reducing pollinator access;
- the same attraction signal can recruit pollinators and antagonists;
- observed net selection can therefore conceal opposing causal channels;
- heterogeneous route evidence cannot be inserted directly into one system-specific model;
- explicit mechanism and scale declarations are necessary before comparative or evolutionary interpretation.

This framework can guide experiments by specifying which quantities must be manipulated or measured to identify each mechanism term.

## Recommended Introduction structure

### Paragraph 1 — Biological problem

Flowers must obtain mutualistic services while remaining exposed to consumers, florivores, predators, and exploiters. Floral traits can therefore affect several interaction partners at once. Introduce attraction and defence/barrier traits as potentially multifunctional rather than intrinsically cooperative or antagonistic.

### Paragraph 2 — What is already known

Summarize evidence that:

- pollinators and herbivores jointly and non-additively affect plant fitness;
- floral signals can recruit both pollinators and antagonists;
- traits or associated organisms that reduce antagonists can also alter pollinator behaviour;
- multivariate selection can favour trait combinations.

Use Herrera et al. (2002), Theis and Adler (2012), Knauer et al. (2018), and Lande and Arnold (1983) here.

### Paragraph 3 — The gap

State that existing evidence usually estimates net effects, selection on individual traits, or system-specific outcomes. It does not by itself identify how one defence/barrier changes the marginal fitness return to one attraction trait, nor separate antagonist relief, mutualist interference, and direct joint cost.

### Paragraph 4 — Why existing context-dependence language is insufficient

Explain that saying the relationship is context dependent does not provide a sign criterion or directional prediction. Also state that mixed curvature is not automatically equivalent to covariance or coevolution.

### Paragraph 5 — Present contribution

Introduce the focal-pair framework, orientation gate, mechanism non-identifiability, general environmental derivative balance, and finite sensitivity analysis. Describe the literature registry only as preliminary route-level context.

### Final Introduction paragraph — Questions and predictions

1. Under what local mechanism balance are attraction and defence complementary versus substitutable?
2. Under which derivative conditions does greater antagonist pressure or pollinator service shift that sign?
3. Does the conditional sign persist across the declared endpoint-normalized response shapes and parameter scenarios?
4. Is the collateral defence-to-pollinator-cost route biologically plausible in at least some systems?

## Recommended Discussion structure

1. restate the local sign criterion, not the grid percentages;
2. distinguish mechanism diagnosis from mechanism identification;
3. interpret the finite sweep as sensitivity, not prevalence;
4. connect the negative defence-to-pollinator route cautiously;
5. explain experimental requirements for estimating channel-specific curvatures;
6. discuss extensions to evolutionary dynamics only as future work.

## Empirical design implied by the theory

A strong empirical test needs a factorial manipulation of the same focal `A` and `D`, measurement of mutualist service and antagonist loss, and a declared fitness scale. Ideally it should estimate:

- `D`'s effect on the marginal mutualist return to `A`;
- `D`'s effect on the marginal antagonist cost associated with `A`;
- direct construction/allocation cost curvature;
- how those quantities change across pollinator-service and antagonist-pressure environments.

Without those measurements, the data may show a total interaction but cannot uniquely assign its biological mechanism.

## Positioning verdict

The project is potentially publishable as a compact theoretical/conceptual paper if it is presented as:

> a rigorous local inference framework for interpreting attraction–defence interactions under multiple biotic agents.

It is weak if presented as:

> proof that attraction and defence commonly coevolve, a universal ecological law, or an empirically validated regime map.

The strongest paper is therefore narrower, more explicit about assumptions, and more useful as a guide to experimental design.
