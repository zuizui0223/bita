# Introduction blueprint

## Paragraph 1 — Biological problem

Animal-pollinated flowers must attract mutualists while remaining exposed to florivores, herbivores, predators, and exploiters. The same floral feature may influence several interaction partners, so attraction and defence cannot be assumed to be independent modules or an intrinsic trade-off.

**Function:** establish the broad problem without claiming novelty.

## Paragraph 2 — Established evidence

Introduce four foundations:

1. multivariate selection can favour combinations of traits rather than isolated traits;
2. pollinators and herbivores can interact non-additively in their effects on plant fitness;
3. attractive floral signals can recruit antagonists as well as pollinators;
4. antagonist suppression can alter the net fitness consequences of floral signals and pollinator deterrence.

Anchor examples:

- Lande & Arnold 1983 — multivariate selection;
- Herrera et al. 2002 — non-additive pollinator × herbivore fitness effects;
- Theis & Adler 2012 — floral fragrance recruits florivores and reduces reproduction;
- Knauer et al. 2018 — florivore removal changes the net role of crab spiders in floral-signal evolution.

**Function:** show that the component biological routes are real and already recognized.

## Paragraph 3 — Inferential gap

Existing work commonly estimates net selection, the effect of one interacting guild, or a system-specific outcome. Such evidence does not automatically identify how a flower-specific defence/barrier changes the marginal fitness return to one focal attraction trait. A total interaction term also does not uniquely identify whether the source is antagonist relief, mutualist interference, or direct joint cost.

**Function:** move from known biology to the specific unresolved inference problem.

## Paragraph 4 — Why “context dependence” is insufficient

A verbal statement that the result depends on ecological context gives no derivative condition for the direction of change. Stronger antagonist pressure does not necessarily move the focal interaction toward complementarity if it also changes mutualist interference or joint cost. Likewise, a local fitness interaction is not automatically a trait covariance, coevolutionary pattern, or evolutionary endpoint.

**Function:** explain why a formal but bounded framework is needed.

## Paragraph 5 — Present contribution

State that the paper develops a focal-pair local diagnostic framework for one declared attraction trait `A`, one declared defence/barrier trait `D`, and one declared outcome scale `W`. The framework:

- separates signed bookkeeping from biological identification;
- requires an orientation gate before assigning non-negative mechanism labels;
- decomposes the local balance into antagonist relief, mutualist interference, and direct joint-cost curvature;
- derives the unrestricted environmental derivative balance before restricted special cases;
- enforces boundaries between route existence, channel curvature, total mixed partial, trait covariance, and evolutionary dynamics.

**Function:** present the actual novelty without claiming a new mixed partial or a first demonstration of mutualist–antagonist interactions.

## Final paragraph — Questions

1. When is the local attraction–defence interaction complementary versus substitutable?
2. Under what derivative conditions do pollinator service and antagonist pressure move that sign?
3. Does the conditional result persist across the declared endpoint-normalized response shapes and biological parameter scenarios?
4. Is a collateral defence-to-pollinator-cost route biologically plausible in at least some systems?

## Recommended one-sentence significance statement

> Floral attraction and defence need not form a fixed trade-off: their local relationship depends on identifiable causal channels, and distinguishing those channels requires stronger inference than either net fitness effects or heterogeneous route evidence alone can provide.

## Recommended novelty statement

> We provide a focal-trait inference framework that makes the assumptions behind mechanistic interpretation explicit and derives the conditions under which ecological change shifts attraction and defence toward local complementarity or substitutability.

## Avoid

- “the first model showing that pollinators and herbivores jointly shape floral evolution”;
- “a new form of correlational selection”;
- “the literature validates the regime map”;
- “the model predicts positive or negative trait covariance”;
- “the grid frequencies estimate prevalence in nature”.
