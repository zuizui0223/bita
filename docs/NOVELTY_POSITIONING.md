# Novelty and positioning boundary

## What is not claimed as novel

The manuscript does not claim novelty for the general observations that floral traits can be shaped by both mutualists and antagonists, that attractive signals can also expose plants to enemies, or that defensive traits can carry collateral costs for beneficial visitors. Those biological ideas are established in the floral-evolution and plant–insect interaction literature.

## The narrower theoretical contribution

The contribution is the explicit local sign criterion for the interaction between floral attraction (A) and flower-specific defence/access limitation (D):

```text
local A x D fitness interaction
= antagonist relief
- mutualist interference
- direct joint cost
```

This framing does three things that are easy to blur in verbal accounts.

1. It does not assume an attraction–defence trade-off. The sign is derived from competing biological channels.
2. It distinguishes ecological interference from a direct allocation or joint-cost trade-off.
3. It gives an explicit break-even boundary: local complementarity occurs only when antagonist relief exceeds mutualist interference plus direct joint cost.

The implemented exponential/linear model is a corollary of this decomposition, not the source of the general claim. The numerical robustness grid tests how stable the sign is to selected nonlinear response functions and parameter choices.

## Relation to existing ecological theory

Existing work establishes several ingredients used here: non-pollinating organisms can select on floral traits; floral signals can attract both mutualists and antagonists; florivory links pollination and herbivory; and mutualistic and antagonistic effects can change ecological outcomes. The present framework should therefore be positioned as a synthesis and formalization of these ingredients at the level of a two-trait local fitness interaction, not as discovery of those ingredients themselves.

The novelty claim should remain at the following level:

> A general local criterion that separates the mechanisms making attraction and defence fitness complements from those making them fitness substitutes, and identifies the break-even condition between those regimes.

## What the criterion does not establish

The sign of the mixed partial is not, by itself, a prediction of:

- population-level trait covariance;
- genetic correlation;
- correlated evolutionary response;
- coevolutionary history;
- a global optimum or stable evolutionary endpoint.

Those require additional assumptions about the fitness landscape, genetic architecture, environmental variation, and evolutionary dynamics.

## Empirical role of the literature layer

The literature layer is not presented as a full empirical test of the criterion. Its present role is narrower: to show that at least one sign-changing pathway in the criterion—defence/access limitation imposing a cost on pollinator use—occurs in real systems.

Evidence for that pathway establishes biological plausibility, but the complete mixed partial would require all relevant channel strengths to be estimated on a compatible scale and context.

## Reviewer-facing positioning

A defensible abstract-level novelty sentence is:

> We derive a local sign criterion showing that floral attraction and defence become fitness complements when antagonist relief exceeds the combined effects of mutualist interference and direct joint cost, and fitness substitutes otherwise.

Avoid stronger formulations such as:

- "We show for the first time that pollinators and herbivores jointly shape floral traits."
- "We predict positive or negative attraction–defence correlations from the mixed partial alone."
- "We empirically validate the complete complementarity–substitutability regime map."

These would overstate either prior-art novelty or the current empirical evidence.
