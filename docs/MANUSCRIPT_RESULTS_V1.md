# Manuscript Results v1

## Results

### Literature synthesis identified evidence structure rather than universal pooled parameters

The evidence synthesis did not support a single universal pooled estimate for the
full attraction–defence framework. Directly comparable effect estimates were sparse
once studies were required to share a compatible trait class, outcome class, effect
metric, design class, and independent study cluster. The clearest cross-study signal
remained a directional `c_D`-like result: in at least some chemical-barrier systems,
defence or access-restriction traits are associated with reduced pollinator use. By
contrast, the remaining routes were not sufficiently exchangeable for universal
pooled estimates. This result motivated treating the literature synthesis as an
evidence map and generalisation boundary, rather than as the central numerical test
of the framework.

Several candidate `d_A` routes also failed source-contract checks. Some sources were
abstract-only, some reported fruit set as the response with floral traits and
antagonist pressure as parallel predictors, and others described routes opposite to
the required trait-to-antagonism direction. Thus, the limiting factor was not simply
the number of papers mentioning floral traits, pollination, or antagonists. The
limiting factor was the rarity of studies estimating the same direct route with a
compatible response and effect metric.

### The *Impatiens* archive provided a linked empirical case study

The title-validated *Impatiens capensis* archive provided the most complete linked
single-system empirical panel currently available in the project. The panel links
early flower redness, early floral condensed tannins, pollinator use, natural floral
damage, reproductive components, flowering phenology, and randomized supplemental
robbing, florivory, and pollination treatments. We therefore treated this system as
the empirical core and analysed it as a response-scale case study rather than as a
source of universal attraction–defence parameters.

The channel models used the archived response scales. Pollinator use was analysed as
a 60-minute-standardized visit count/rate. Natural floral damage was analysed as an
individual-flower tissue-loss fraction, with plant-cluster robust inference. Seeds
per chasmogamous fruit were analysed as individual-fruit seed counts, again with
plant-cluster robust inference. In the raw flower and fruit models, plant-level
predictors were standardized once per plant before expansion to repeated flowers or
fruits, so that plants with more recorded flowers or fruits did not define the
one-standard-deviation trait contrast.

### Floral tannins were associated with lower pollinator use, but not with resolved protection from natural floral damage

The floral-tannin candidate showed a negative association with pollinator use. A one
standard deviation increase in log1p early floral condensed tannins was associated
with a lower 60-minute-standardized pollinator-use rate (RR = 0.682, 95% CI
0.479–0.972). This result is consistent with a pollination-cost-like `c_D` route in
this system.

This pollination-cost-like association was not matched by evidence for a protective
association against natural floral damage. In the flower-level fractional-logit
model, the tannin candidate was not resolved as reducing natural tissue loss (OR =
1.121, 95% CI 0.990–1.269). The point estimate was not in the predicted protective
direction. Thus, for this D candidate, the response-scale channel map shows a
pollinator-use cost-like association but no resolved antagonism-suppression benefit.

Flower redness was not resolved as a predictor of either pollinator use or natural
floral damage. The estimated association between early flower redness and pollinator
use was weak and imprecise (RR = 0.981, 95% CI 0.626–1.539). The association between
early flower redness and natural floral damage was also unresolved (OR = 1.064, 95%
CI 0.933–1.213). These results do not show that attraction is biologically irrelevant
in this system; they show that the present archived panel and declared adjustment set
do not isolate a precise flower-redness route to either pollinator use or natural
floral damage.

### Zero-heavy pollinator data required a model-adequacy sensitivity, not model replacement

The pollinator-use response contained substantial zero mass. Among the 81 complete
plants in the primary pollinator-rate model, 52 had zero observed visits, and the
primary model showed high overdispersion (Pearson dispersion = 23.18). We therefore
reported a declared post-primary hurdle sensitivity to separate visit occurrence
from positive visit intensity.

The hurdle sensitivity did not resolve a tannin association with whether any visit
was observed (OR = 0.780, 95% CI 0.511–1.191). However, among plants with at least
one observed visit, the tannin candidate was associated with lower positive visit
intensity (RR = 0.515, 95% CI 0.332–0.799). This pattern suggests that the primary
pollinator-cost-like association is compatible with reduced intensity among visited
plants, rather than being resolved solely as a presence–absence effect. Because this
hurdle analysis was triggered by model adequacy, it is interpreted as a diagnostic
sensitivity and not as a replacement for the primary pollinator-rate result.

### The A × D candidate seed surface was unresolved

The reproductive-component model for seeds per chasmogamous fruit did not resolve an
interaction between early flower redness and the floral-tannin candidate. The
estimated A × D candidate association was close to one on the rate-ratio scale (RR =
1.008, 95% CI 0.968–1.049). This result does not support a detectable A × D
component surface for seeds per chasmogamous fruit in the archived panel.

This seed result is a reproductive-component analysis rather than a total-fitness
surface. It therefore does not estimate the Part A mixed partial and does not
calibrate a shared allocation cost. The result is best interpreted as a component
constraint: the archived seed-count data do not reveal a strong A × D candidate
surface under the declared model.

### Randomized imposed florivory reduced chasmogamous fruit production

The randomized treatment analysis provided a separate line of evidence about the
reproductive consequences of floral damage. The processed table contained all 200
plants in valid treatment-assignment cells, with exactly 25 plants in each of the
eight Robbing × Florivory × Pollination combinations. The descriptive
baseline-complete audit retained 170 plants, with 19–24 plants per cell. The
randomized downstream analysis therefore did not depend on missing treatment cells or
extreme assignment imbalance.

In the full effect-coded 2 × 2 × 2 factorial model, with all two-way and three-way
treatment interactions retained and pre-treatment phenology included, supplemental
florivory reduced chasmogamous fruit production. The assigned florivory contrast had
a negative effect on chasmogamous fruits per plant per day (standardized coefficient
= -0.290, 95% CI -0.540 to -0.040). This is the clearest experimental result in the
case study: imposed floral damage reduced a reproductive component.

The same randomized florivory assignment did not resolve an effect on seeds per
chasmogamous fruit (standardized coefficient = -0.142, 95% CI -0.622 to 0.338). Thus,
the experimental cost of imposed florivory was evident for fruit production but not
for seed number per chasmogamous fruit in the declared factorial models.

### Phenology was a major axis of reproductive variation

Phenology was strongly associated with chasmogamous fruit production. In the full
factorial fruit model, later first chasmogamous flowering was associated with lower
chasmogamous fruits per plant per day (standardized coefficient = -0.493, 95% CI
-0.617 to -0.369). This association was stronger and more precise than any observed
flower-redness channel in the response-scale models.

This phenology result matters for interpretation. It indicates that seasonal timing
is not merely a nuisance covariate in this system. Any future test of attraction,
defence, pollination, and antagonism in *Impatiens* should treat flowering time as a
central ecological axis, especially when linking floral traits to reproductive
components.

### Empirical channel ledger

Taken together, the *Impatiens* results form an asymmetric empirical ledger. The
floral-tannin candidate is associated with reduced pollinator use, but not with a
resolved reduction in natural floral damage. Flower redness is unresolved for both
pollinator use and natural floral damage. The A × D candidate surface for seeds per
chasmogamous fruit is unresolved. In contrast, experimentally imposed florivory
reduces chasmogamous fruit production.

The resulting case study therefore supports only part of the theoretical route
structure. It supports a pollination-cost-like association for a defence-like floral
chemistry candidate and a downstream experimental cost of floral damage. It does not
identify the corresponding defensive benefit, the attraction-mediated antagonism
route, a total-fitness A × D surface, or the shared-cost term. The empirical result is
therefore not a direct demonstration of complementarity or substitutability. Instead,
it is a constrained single-system test showing which parts of the attraction–defence
framework are visible, unresolved, or not identified in one linked biological panel.
