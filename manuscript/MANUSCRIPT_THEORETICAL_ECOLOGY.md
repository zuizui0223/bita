# When are floral attraction and defence complementary? A local mechanistic diagnostic under mutualists, antagonists, and direct joint costs

## Abstract

Flowers must attract mutualists while remaining exposed to florivores, nectar robbers, pathogens, and other antagonists. Attraction and flower-specific defence are therefore unlikely to form either a universal trade-off or a universal complementary syndrome. We develop a local diagnostic framework for one declared floral attraction trait, one declared flower-specific trait with an operationally defined antagonist-reduction role, and one declared fitness or biological-outcome scale. The focal quantity is the mixed partial of the outcome surface with respect to attraction and defence. After explicit orientation conditions are established, its sign is a balance among antagonist relief, interference with legitimate pollinators, and direct joint-cost curvature. Positive local curvature means that either trait strengthens the marginal effect of the other on the declared scale; it does not require either trait to have a positive marginal effect by itself. The same total curvature can be generated by different allocations among the three channels, so even noiseless observation of total fitness does not identify mechanism. We further derive the unrestricted environmental derivative balance and show that increased antagonist pressure or pollinator service has no universal directional effect unless additional local inequalities are satisfied. An endpoint-normalized finite sensitivity analysis of an implemented corollary produced both complementary and substitutable regimes across 2,592 declared evaluations, with strong within-scenario agreement under contrasting biological scenarios but no unanimity across the deliberately heterogeneous full tested set. These results are sensitivity diagnostics, not estimates of prevalence in nature. The framework clarifies which measurements and manipulations are required to distinguish guarded attraction from pollinator interference and direct joint costs, while separating local fitness curvature from trait covariance, genetic correlation, optima, and evolutionary dynamics.

**Keywords:** correlational selection; floral defence; florivory; mutualism; pollination; trait integration

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Ecological problem

Flowers interact simultaneously with organisms that increase reproduction and organisms that diminish it. Signals, rewards, and floral structures recruit pollinators, but the same flowers are exposed to florivores, nectar robbers, and pathogens. Traits that influence attraction or access can therefore alter several interaction pathways at once. Attraction and defence cannot be understood independently when the fitness effect of either trait depends on how the other changes pollinator service, antagonist damage, or their joint costs.

### 1.2 Existing theories

Existing theory provides several complementary ways to study such multivariate phenotypes. Correlational selection quantifies whether particular combinations of trait values are associated with fitness beyond their separate effects, thereby describing cross-trait selection on a specified scale (Lande and Arnold 1983; Phillips and Arnold 1989; Blows and Brooks 2003). Fitness-landscape approaches characterize local curvature, peaks, valleys, and the placement of phenotypes on a multivariate outcome surface. Trait-interaction models ask whether the effect of one trait depends on the value of another. Interaction-modification studies ask how one ecological interaction changes the consequences of another, as when herbivory alters pollination or an antagonist indirectly changes floral-signal evolution (Herrera et al. 2002; Knauer et al. 2018). These approaches establish that floral traits and ecological interactions need not act additively, and they provide the appropriate language for describing multivariate selection and context dependence.

They answer different questions from the one addressed here. Correlational selection identifies total cross-trait fitness curvature under a declared parameterization; it does not, by itself, determine which ecological pathway generated that curvature. A fitness landscape describes the outcome surface but does not uniquely allocate its shape among pollinator benefits, antagonist losses, and direct costs. Trait-interaction models detect conditional effects, while interaction-modification studies establish that ecological partners can alter one another's consequences. None of these limitations is a defect in the existing theories. Rather, each framework is designed for a different inferential target.

### 1.3 Ecological inference gap

The unresolved ecological question is: **When does one attraction trait and one defence trait become locally complementary rather than substitutable?** Complementarity here concerns whether either trait strengthens the marginal fitness effect of the other in a specified local ecological context; substitutability concerns whether it weakens that marginal effect. This question is narrower than asking whether the two traits covary, whether correlational selection is present, or where a fitness optimum lies.

The distinction matters because the same observed cross-trait fitness pattern can support opposing ecological explanations. A positive local interaction may arise because defence protects the additional reproductive value generated by attraction. A negative local interaction may arise because defence obstructs legitimate pollinator use. Direct construction, allocation, or physiological costs can further alter the relationship. Consequently, observing trait covariance, estimating a total fitness surface, or detecting a significant trait interaction does not establish whether attraction and defence are ecologically complementary, why they are so, or how that relationship should change with pollinator service or antagonist pressure. A diagnostic theory is necessary not to replace correlational selection or fitness-landscape analysis, but to connect their total cross-trait signal to the specific ecological balance that gives that signal its interpretation.

### 1.4 Biological hypothesis

Our biological hypothesis is conditional. A flower-specific defence trait may reduce antagonist damage, thereby preserving the fitness returns generated by attraction. The same defence trait may also reduce pollinator use by limiting access, changing reward consumption, or otherwise interfering with legitimate visitors. Attraction and defence may additionally share direct construction, allocation, or physiological costs. Evidence that floral signals can recruit antagonists, that resistance can impose pollination costs, and that floral chemistry can have variable effects on pollinators makes each route biologically plausible (Strauss et al. 1999; Theis and Adler 2012; Wright et al. 2013; Richardson et al. 2015; Stevenson et al. 2017).

These routes imply neither universal complementarity nor universal antagonism. Attraction and defence are locally complementary only when the benefit of antagonist reduction dominates pollinator interference and shared direct costs on the declared outcome scale. They are locally substitutable when the opposing effects dominate. The relationship must therefore be evaluated for a specified pair of traits, a specified fitness or biological outcome, and a specified ecological context.

### 1.5 Contributions

This paper makes four contributions.

1. **Local diagnostic.** We provide a focal-trait diagnostic that classifies one declared attraction trait and one declared flower-specific antagonist-reducing trait as locally complementary or substitutable on a declared outcome scale, while keeping this classification separate from trait covariance, genetic correlation, optima, and evolutionary dynamics.

2. **Mechanistic decomposition.** We decompose the total local interaction into antagonist relief, pollinator interference, and direct joint-cost curvature, state the orientation conditions required for that biological reading, and show why total fitness curvature alone does not identify the underlying mechanism.

3. **Environmental criterion.** We derive the unrestricted criterion governing how the local attraction-defence relationship changes with antagonist pressure and pollinator service, showing that environmental direction cannot be inferred from pressure alone without comparing all channel responses.

4. **Finite robustness analysis.** We use an endpoint-normalized finite sensitivity analysis of the implemented corollary to evaluate whether conditional sign classifications persist across declared biological scenarios and alternative response shapes, without interpreting finite-grid occupancies as frequencies in nature.

### 1.6 Paper organization

Section 2 defines the focal traits and outcome scale, develops the signed channel decomposition and orientation criterion, establishes mechanism non-identifiability, derives the environmental comparative statics, and specifies the finite sensitivity design. Section 3 presents the local sign criterion, the environmental results, and the sensitivity-analysis outcomes. Section 4 discusses the ecological interpretation, inference limits, and empirical requirements of the framework. Section 5 summarizes the conclusions.

## 2. Model and analytical framework

### 2.1 Focal traits and outcome scale

Let \(A\) denote one measured floral attraction trait, \(D\) one measured flower-specific trait with an operationally defined antagonist-reduction role, and \(W(A,D;E)\) a declared fitness or biological-outcome score under ecological context \(E\). We use defence/access limitation only for a focal flower-specific trait with such an antagonist-reduction role; collateral obstruction of legitimate pollinators is a possible effect of that same trait, not the definition of defence. Neither \(A\) nor \(D\) is an omnibus index. Each application must declare its biological measurement scale.

The local attraction-defence interaction is

\[
W_{AD}=\frac{\partial^2 W}{\partial A\,\partial D}.
\]

On the declared coordinates and outcome scale, \(W_{AD}>0\) means that increasing either trait strengthens the local marginal effect of the other. We call this local complementarity. \(W_{AD}<0\) means that increasing either trait weakens the local marginal effect of the other, which we call local substitutability. These labels describe local curvature only. In particular, \(W_{AD}>0\) does not imply that either first derivative \(W_A\) or \(W_D\) is positive.

Positive affine rescaling of either trait preserves the sign of a nonzero mixed partial. Arbitrary nonlinear transformations need not. The sign is therefore a property of the declared biological parameterization rather than a transformation-free classification.

### 2.2 Signed channel decomposition

Write total outcome as mutualist contribution \(M\), antagonist loss \(G\), and a direct cost channel \(C\):

\[
W=M-G-C.
\]

Differentiation gives the signed identity

\[
W_{AD}=M_{AD}-G_{AD}-C_{AD}.
\]

Here \(C_{AD}\) is direct joint-cost curvature. Direct construction, allocation, or physiological costs are possible biological sources of this term, but the mathematical object is the cross-curvature of the declared cost channel, not total energetic cost.

This identity is algebraic. Biological labels do not determine the signs of the component mixed partials. Showing that \(D\) lowers pollinator visitation does not alone establish \(M_{AD}<0\); it must lower the marginal mutualist return to the same focal \(A\). Likewise, showing that \(D\) reduces antagonist damage does not alone establish \(G_{AD}<0\); it must reduce the marginal antagonist cost associated with the same focal \(A\).

### 2.3 Orientation gate and local sign criterion

Within a neighbourhood where the focal model establishes

\[
M_{AD}\le 0,\qquad G_{AD}\le 0,\qquad C_{AD}\ge 0,
\]

define non-negative magnitudes

\[
\iota=-M_{AD},\qquad \rho=-G_{AD},\qquad \kappa=C_{AD}.
\]

Then

\[
W_{AD}=\rho-\iota-\kappa.
\]

The local sign criterion is therefore

\[
W_{AD}>0 \iff \rho>\iota+\kappa,
\]

with equality defining the local break-even boundary. This oriented form is valid only after the orientation gate has been justified for the focal application.

### 2.4 Mechanism non-identifiability

**Proposition 1.** Observation of total \(W(A,D)\), including exact knowledge of \(W_{AD}\), does not uniquely identify \(M_{AD}\), \(G_{AD}\), and \(C_{AD}\).

**Argument.** For any smooth function \(Q(A,D)\), define

\[
M^*=M+Q,\qquad G^*=G+Q,\qquad C^*=C.
\]

Then \(M^*-G^*-C^*=W\), while \(M^*_{AD}=M_{AD}+Q_{AD}\) and \(G^*_{AD}=G_{AD}+Q_{AD}\). The observed total outcome surface is unchanged although the channel curvatures differ. Equivalent reallocations can be constructed involving \(C\). This proves structural non-identifiability from total \(W\), even under noiseless and complete observation of that surface. It does not imply non-identifiability after channel-specific interventions, measurements, or justified structural restrictions.

### 2.5 Environmental comparative statics

Let pollinator service \(P\) and antagonist pressure \(H\) be exogenous context indices. In an oriented neighbourhood,

\[
W_{AD}(P,H)=\rho(P,H)-\iota(P,H)-\kappa(P,H).
\]

Thus,

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial H}
=\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial H}
-\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial H}
-\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial H},
\]

and

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial P}
=\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial P}
-\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial P}
-\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial P}.
\]

The explicit local directional conditions are

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial H}>0
\iff
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial H}
>
\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial H}
+
\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial H},
\]

and

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial P}<0
\iff
\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial P}
+
\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial P}
>
\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial P}.
\]

Equalities define local environmental break-even conditions, and reverse inequalities reverse the direction. Greater antagonist pressure therefore moves the interaction toward complementarity only under the first inequality. Greater pollinator service moves the interaction toward substitutability only under the second. Verbal claims about more antagonists or more pollinators are insufficient without these conditions.

### 2.6 Implemented corollary and finite sensitivity analysis

The implemented corollary uses declared nonlinear response functions for attraction, defence, mutualist service, antagonist damage, and direct joint cost. Four response-shape variants were endpoint-normalized over \(A,D\in[0,1]\), so attraction response at \(A=1\), defence response at \(D=1\), and direct joint-cost scale at \(A=D=1\) match across variants.

The finite design evaluated \(A,D\in\{0.2,0.5,0.8\}\), pollinator-service and antagonist-pressure indices \(P,H\in\{0.2,0.5,0.8\}\), and an auxiliary reproductive-assurance moderator \(R\in\{0,0.5\}\). \(R\) is not a third focal trait. Four biological parameter scenarios and four endpoint-normalized response-shape variants yielded 2,592 mixed-partial evaluations.

Analytic mixed partials were checked against independently implemented finite-difference derivatives. The absolute numerical tolerance for classifying zero was \(10^{-10}\) on the declared score scale. Finite-grid percentages are descriptive occupancies under the declared design, not probabilities or estimates of prevalence in nature.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 General sign criterion

The oriented local interaction was complementary exactly when antagonist relief exceeded the sum of mutualist interference and direct joint-cost curvature. It was substitutable when the opposing terms exceeded antagonist relief. This result did not require attraction and defence to have a fixed relationship, and complementarity did not imply that both traits had positive marginal effects.

### 3.2 Environmental direction cannot be inferred from pressure alone

The unrestricted derivatives showed that increased antagonist pressure did not necessarily increase complementarity, because antagonist pressure could also modify pollinator interference or direct joint-cost curvature. Similarly, increased pollinator service did not necessarily favour substitutability. Directional predictions required the explicit inequalities among all context derivatives given above.

### 3.3 Finite sensitivity analysis

Across the 2,592 declared evaluations, 1,342 were complementary and 1,250 were substitutable; none fell within the numerical zero tolerance. These correspond to unweighted finite-grid occupancies of 51.8% and 48.2%, respectively, and must not be interpreted as natural frequencies.

Across 648 fixed case-by-parameter-scenario summaries, 480 were unanimous across the four response-shape variants and 168 were mixed or sensitive. In the high-antagonism-tracking, low-pollination-obstruction, low-direct-joint-cost scenario, 617 of 648 evaluations were complementary and 144 of 162 local cases were unanimous across response shapes. In the high-pollination-obstruction and high-direct-joint-cost scenario, 610 of 648 evaluations were substitutable and 138 of 162 local cases were unanimous.

After combining all four deliberately contrasting biological scenarios and response shapes, none of the 162 local cases was unanimous across the full tested set. This is expected for a conditional theory in which biologically decisive route strengths are deliberately varied.

## 4. Discussion

Our framework shows why floral attraction and flower-specific defence cannot be assigned a universal relationship. The same defence trait can increase the marginal value of attraction by blocking enemies, decrease it by obstructing legitimate pollinators, and interact with attraction through direct joint-cost curvature. The sign of the total local interaction is therefore a balance among channels rather than an intrinsic property of the labels attraction and defence.

The first contribution is inferential. A measured total fitness surface, even if estimated without error, cannot uniquely reveal the biological source of its mixed curvature. This structural non-identifiability is not solved by a larger sample of total fitness observations. It requires channel-specific interventions or additional structural assumptions. Experiments should manipulate the same focal \(A\) and \(D\), quantify mutualist service and antagonist loss separately, and measure the direct cost channel where possible.

The second contribution is to context dependence. Ecological studies often conclude that outcomes depend on pollinator or antagonist abundance, but abundance alone does not determine the direction of change in \(W_{AD}\). Increased antagonist pressure favours local complementarity only if the increase in relief curvature dominates simultaneous changes in interference and direct joint-cost curvature. This qualification matters in systems where antagonists alter pollinator behaviour, where pollinators act as pathogen vectors, or where defence expression itself changes with stress.

The finite sensitivity analysis illustrates rather than validates the theory. Contrasting biological scenarios generated opposite sign-dominant regimes, while endpoint normalization showed that many within-scenario classifications persisted across alternative response shapes. The absence of unanimity across the full deliberately heterogeneous design is not model failure; it is the expected signature of a conditional sign boundary. The grid does not estimate how common either sign is in nature.

The framework is related to correlational selection but should not be conflated with evolutionary prediction. Under standardized trait and relative-fitness coordinates, \(W_{AD}\) may correspond to a correlational selection term. Predicting phenotypic covariance, genetic correlation, trajectories, or equilibria additionally requires genetic architecture, constraints, inheritance, and dynamics. Positive local curvature can favour combinations locally without guaranteeing positive population-level covariance.

The current literature establishes route plausibility but not the complete focal mixed partial. Floral signals can recruit antagonists, resistance can impose pollination costs, nectar chemistry can either deter or benefit pollinators, and mutualists and antagonists can have non-additive fitness effects. The retained repository registry is abstract-level, single-coder context with no eligible quantitative effect rows; it is neither a systematic review nor empirical validation or parameter calibration. Heterogeneous studies across traits and taxa cannot be pooled into one system-specific \(D\) axis or used to validate the regime map.

A decisive empirical test would use a factorial manipulation of one attraction trait and one antagonist-reducing defence trait under replicated pollinator-service and antagonist-pressure contexts. Mutualist contribution, antagonist loss, the direct cost channel, and total fitness should be measured separately. Such a design would estimate both the total interaction and the channel-specific curvatures needed to distinguish antagonist relief from mutualist interference.

## 5. Conclusions

Floral attraction and defence are locally complementary when antagonist relief exceeds pollinator interference and direct joint-cost curvature, and locally substitutable when the opposing contributions dominate. The value of this criterion lies less in naming a mixed partial than in making its mechanism, orientation assumptions, environmental derivatives, and inference limits explicit. The framework converts a broad verbal claim of context dependence into a set of measurable local conditions and experimental requirements.
