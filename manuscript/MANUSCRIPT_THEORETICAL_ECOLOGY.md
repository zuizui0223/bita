# When are floral attraction and defence complementary? A local mechanistic diagnostic under mutualists, antagonists, and joint costs

## Abstract

Flowers must attract mutualists while remaining exposed to florivores, nectar robbers, pathogens, and other antagonists. Attraction and flower-specific defence are therefore unlikely to form a universal trade-off or a universal complementary syndrome. We develop a local diagnostic framework for one declared floral attraction trait, one declared flower-specific defence or access-limitation trait, and one declared fitness or biological-outcome scale. The focal quantity is the mixed partial of the outcome surface with respect to attraction and defence. We show that its sign can be interpreted, after explicit orientation conditions are established, as a balance among antagonist relief, interference with legitimate pollinators, and direct joint-cost curvature. Positive local curvature indicates that either trait strengthens the marginal effect of the other on the declared scale; negative curvature indicates local substitutability. The same total curvature can, however, be generated by different allocations among the three channels, so total fitness alone does not identify mechanism. We further derive the unrestricted environmental derivative balance and show that increased antagonist pressure or pollinator service has no universal directional effect unless additional local inequalities are satisfied. An endpoint-normalized finite sensitivity analysis of an implemented corollary produced both complementary and substitutable regimes across 2,592 declared evaluations, with strong within-scenario agreement in contrasting biological scenarios but no unanimity across the full deliberately heterogeneous tested set. These results are sensitivity diagnostics, not estimates of prevalence in nature. The framework clarifies which measurements and manipulations are required to distinguish guarded attraction from pollinator interference and shared costs, while separating local fitness curvature from trait covariance, genetic correlation, optima, and evolutionary dynamics.

**Keywords:** correlational selection; floral defence; florivory; mutualism; pollination; trait integration

## 1. Introduction

Animal-pollinated flowers function simultaneously as signals, rewards, and mechanical interfaces through which plants obtain mutualistic services. Yet flowers are also exposed to florivores, herbivores, nectar robbers, seed predators, pathogens, and other organisms that can reduce reproductive success. Floral colour, scent, size, display, reward, and morphology may therefore affect several interaction partners at once, and their fitness consequences cannot be understood solely from pollinator attraction. Pollinator-mediated selection on individual floral traits and on combinations of traits is well established, as are the multivariate-selection concepts used to describe selection on correlated characters (Lande and Arnold 1983; Fenster et al. 2004; Harder and Johnson 2009; Schiestl and Johnson 2013). However, floral integration or phenotypic covariance may also arise from developmental covariance, shared genetic architecture, or phylogenetic constraint. Observed trait correlations therefore do not, by themselves, demonstrate adaptive correlational selection or identify the ecological mechanism producing a particular trait combination (Armbruster et al. 2014).

Non-pollinating organisms can also impose strong selection on floral phenotypes. Florivory removes reproductive tissue directly, but it can additionally modify floral display, scent, reward, and morphology, thereby altering pollinator visitation, pollen transfer, and plant reproduction (Krupnick et al. 1999; Mothershead and Marquis 2000; McCall and Irwin 2006). Signals that recruit mutualists may also be exploited by antagonists. In *Cucurbita pepo*, experimentally enhanced floral fragrance increased attraction of florivorous beetles and reduced reproduction, demonstrating a direct ecological cost of advertising (Theis and Adler 2012). Such evidence rules out the general assumption that stronger attraction only increases pollination benefits. It does not, however, imply that antagonists invariably weaken selection for attraction, because the outcome may vary with antagonist identity, density, phenology, and community composition.

Flower-specific defence and access-limitation traits are similarly multifunctional. Chemical defences, trichomes, sticky surfaces, narrow floral tubes, mechanical barriers, and secondary metabolites in nectar may deter florivores or exploiters while also altering legitimate pollinator access, reward consumption, learning, residence time, and pollen transfer. An ecological cost of resistance expressed through pollination was demonstrated in *Brassica rapa*, where herbivore-resistant lines differed in floral traits and pollinator use (Strauss et al. 1999). Nevertheless, defence-associated traits do not have uniformly negative effects on pollinators. Nectar secondary metabolites can deter foragers at some concentrations, enhance learning or memory at others, and reduce pathogen loads in pollinators (Wright et al. 2013; Richardson et al. 2015; Stevenson et al. 2017). Thus, a trait labelled as defence cannot generally be treated as a single allocation cost, nor can pollinator interference be inferred from that label alone.

The fitness effects of mutualists and antagonists may also be non-additive. A factorial field experiment manipulating pollination and herbivory showed that their joint effects on plant fitness were not a simple sum and suggested a pathway to correlated evolution of traits involved in mutualistic and antagonistic interactions (Herrera et al. 2002). Likewise, flower-dwelling crab spiders may deter pollinators while indirectly altering floral-signal evolution by removing florivores (Knauer et al. 2018). These studies establish that the net consequences of attraction, defence, and antagonist suppression can depend on the combination of ecological interactions. They therefore make a fixed attraction-defence trade-off implausible as a universal expectation. At the same time, they do not provide a general rule that attraction and defence should instead be positively associated.

The unresolved problem is not whether pollinators and antagonists both matter, but how their known effects combine for one explicitly defined attraction trait and one explicitly defined flower-specific defence or barrier trait. A local mixed partial measures how the marginal effect of one trait changes with the other. This quantity is closely related to cross-trait fitness curvature and, under appropriate trait and relative-fitness scaling, to correlational selection (Lande and Arnold 1983; Phillips and Arnold 1989; Blows and Brooks 2003). It is therefore not a new mathematical object. More importantly, it does not by itself imply phenotypic covariance, genetic correlation, an evolutionary trajectory, an optimum, or an evolutionarily stable strategy.

A second inferential problem is that the same total fitness curvature can arise from different biological mechanisms. A defence trait may increase the marginal value of attraction by reducing the additional antagonist damage generated by stronger attraction. The same defence trait may instead reduce the marginal value of attraction by obstructing legitimate pollinators. Attraction and defence may also share direct construction, allocation, or physiological costs. Total curvature cannot uniquely identify these contributions without channel-specific measurements or structural assumptions.

Here, we develop a focal-trait local diagnostic framework for one declared attraction trait, one declared flower-specific defence or barrier trait, and one declared outcome scale. We separate signed bookkeeping from the additional orientation assumptions required to interpret channel terms as non-negative mechanism magnitudes; derive the local sign condition under which antagonist relief exceeds pollinator interference and joint-cost curvature; establish a formal non-identifiability result; and derive the unrestricted environmental derivative balance before considering separable special cases. We then use an endpoint-normalized finite sensitivity analysis to illustrate conditional sign changes in an implemented corollary. The framework is intentionally narrower than a full evolutionary model.

## 2. Model and analytical framework

### 2.1 Focal traits and outcome scale

Let \(A\) denote one measured floral attraction trait, \(D\) one measured flower-specific defence or access-limitation trait, and \(W(A,D;E)\) a declared fitness or biological-outcome score under ecological context \(E\). Neither \(A\) nor \(D\) is an omnibus index. Each application must declare its biological measurement scale.

The local attraction-defence interaction is

\[
W_{AD}=\frac{\partial^2 W}{\partial A\,\partial D}.
\]

On the declared coordinates and outcome scale, \(W_{AD}>0\) means that increasing either trait strengthens the local marginal effect of the other. We call this local complementarity. \(W_{AD}<0\) means that increasing either trait weakens the local marginal effect of the other, which we call local substitutability. These labels describe local curvature only.

Positive affine rescaling of either trait preserves the sign of a nonzero mixed partial. Arbitrary nonlinear transformations need not. The sign is therefore a property of the declared biological parameterization rather than a transformation-free classification.

### 2.2 Signed channel decomposition

Write total outcome as mutualist contribution \(M\), antagonist loss \(G\), and direct or allocation cost \(C\):

\[
W=M-G-C.
\]

Differentiation gives the signed identity

\[
W_{AD}=M_{AD}-G_{AD}-C_{AD}.
\]

This identity is algebraic. Biological labels do not determine the signs of the component mixed partials. Showing that \(D\) lowers pollinator visitation does not alone establish \(M_{AD}<0\); it must lower the marginal mutualist return to the same focal \(A\). Likewise, showing that \(D\) reduces antagonist damage does not alone establish \(G_{AD}<0\); it must reduce the marginal antagonist cost associated with the same focal \(A\).

### 2.3 Orientation gate and local sign criterion

Within a neighbourhood where the focal model establishes

\[
M_{AD}\le 0,\qquad G_{AD}\le 0,\qquad C_{AD}\ge 0,
\]

define non-negative magnitudes

\[
\iota=-M_{AD},\qquad \rho=-G_{AD},\qquad \kappa=C_{AD}.
\]

Then

\[
W_{AD}=\rho-\iota-\kappa.
\]

The local sign criterion is therefore

\[
W_{AD}>0 \iff \rho>\iota+\kappa,
\]

with equality defining the local break-even boundary. This oriented form is valid only after the orientation gate has been justified for the focal application.

### 2.4 Mechanism non-identifiability

**Proposition 1.** Observation of total \(W(A,D)\), including exact knowledge of \(W_{AD}\), does not uniquely identify \(M_{AD}\), \(G_{AD}\), and \(C_{AD}\).

**Argument.** For any smooth function \(Q(A,D)\), define

\[
M^*=M+Q,\qquad G^*=G+Q,\qquad C^*=C.
\]

Then \(M^*-G^*-C^*=W\), while \(M^*_{AD}=M_{AD}+Q_{AD}\) and \(G^*_{AD}=G_{AD}+Q_{AD}\). The observed total outcome surface is unchanged although the channel curvatures differ. Equivalent reallocations can be constructed involving \(C\). Channel-specific manipulations, measurements, or structural restrictions are therefore required for mechanism identification.

### 2.5 Environmental comparative statics

Let pollinator service \(P\) and antagonist pressure \(H\) be exogenous context indices. In an oriented neighbourhood,

\[
W_{AD}(P,H)=\rho(P,H)-\iota(P,H)-\kappa(P,H).
\]

Thus,

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial H}
=\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial H}
-\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial H}
-\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial H},
\]

and

\[
\frac{\partial W_{AD}}{\partial P}
=\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial P}
-\frac{\partial \iota}{\partial P}
-\frac{\partial \kappa}{\partial P}.
\]

Greater antagonist pressure moves the interaction toward complementarity only when its effect on antagonist relief exceeds its effects on mutualist interference and joint-cost curvature. Greater pollinator service moves the interaction toward substitutability only under the corresponding derivative inequality. Verbal claims about more antagonists or more pollinators are insufficient without these conditions.

### 2.6 Implemented corollary and finite sensitivity analysis

The implemented corollary uses declared nonlinear response functions for attraction, defence, mutualist service, antagonist damage, and direct joint cost. Four response-shape variants were endpoint-normalized over \(A,D\in[0,1]\), so attraction response at \(A=1\), defence response at \(D=1\), and direct joint-cost scale at \(A=D=1\) match across variants.

The finite design evaluated \(A,D\in\{0.2,0.5,0.8\}\), pollinator-service and antagonist-pressure indices \(P,H\in\{0.2,0.5,0.8\}\), and an auxiliary reproductive-assurance moderator \(R\in\{0,0.5\}\). \(R\) is not a third focal trait. Four biological parameter scenarios and four endpoint-normalized response-shape variants yielded 2,592 mixed-partial evaluations.

Analytic mixed partials were checked against independently implemented finite-difference derivatives. The absolute numerical tolerance for classifying zero was \(10^{-10}\) on the declared score scale. Finite-grid percentages are descriptive occupancies under the declared design, not probabilities or estimates of prevalence in nature.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 General sign criterion

The oriented local interaction was complementary exactly when antagonist relief exceeded the sum of mutualist interference and direct joint-cost curvature. It was substitutable when the opposing terms exceeded antagonist relief. This result did not require attraction and defence to have a fixed relationship.

### 3.2 Environmental direction cannot be inferred from pressure alone

The unrestricted derivatives showed that increased antagonist pressure did not necessarily increase complementarity, because antagonist pressure could also modify pollinator interference or joint costs. Similarly, increased pollinator service did not necessarily favour substitutability. Directional predictions required explicit inequalities among all context derivatives.

### 3.3 Finite sensitivity analysis

Across the 2,592 declared evaluations, 1,342 were complementary and 1,250 were substitutable; none fell within the numerical zero tolerance. These correspond to unweighted finite-grid occupancies of 51.8% and 48.2%, respectively, and must not be interpreted as natural frequencies.

Across 648 fixed case-by-parameter-scenario summaries, 480 were unanimous across the four response-shape variants and 168 were mixed or sensitive. In the high-antagonism-tracking, low-pollination-obstruction, low-joint-cost scenario, 617 of 648 evaluations were complementary and 144 of 162 local cases were unanimous across response shapes. In the high-pollination-obstruction and high-joint-cost scenario, 610 of 648 evaluations were substitutable and 138 of 162 local cases were unanimous.

After combining all four deliberately contrasting biological scenarios and response shapes, none of the 162 local cases was unanimous across the full tested set. This is expected for a conditional theory in which biologically decisive route strengths are deliberately varied.

## 4. Discussion

Our framework shows why floral attraction and flower-specific defence cannot be assigned a universal relationship. The same defence trait can increase the marginal value of attraction by blocking enemies, decrease it by obstructing legitimate pollinators, and interact with attraction through construction or physiological costs. The sign of the total local interaction is therefore a balance among channels rather than an intrinsic property of the labels attraction and defence.

The first contribution is inferential. A measured total fitness surface, even if estimated without error, cannot uniquely reveal the biological source of its mixed curvature. This non-identifiability is not solved by a larger sample of total fitness observations. It requires channel-specific interventions or additional structural assumptions. Experiments should manipulate the same focal \(A\) and \(D\), quantify mutualist service and antagonist loss separately, and measure direct cost where possible.

The second contribution is to context dependence. Ecological studies often conclude that outcomes depend on pollinator or antagonist abundance, but abundance alone does not determine the direction of change in \(W_{AD}\). Increased antagonist pressure favours local complementarity only if the increase in relief curvature dominates simultaneous changes in interference and joint cost. This qualification matters in systems where antagonists alter pollinator behaviour, where pollinators act as pathogen vectors, or where defence expression itself changes with stress.

The finite sensitivity analysis illustrates rather than validates the theory. Contrasting biological scenarios generated opposite sign-dominant regimes, while endpoint normalization showed that many within-scenario classifications persisted across alternative response shapes. The absence of unanimity across the full deliberately heterogeneous design is not model failure; it is the expected signature of a conditional sign boundary. The grid does not estimate how common either sign is in nature.

The framework is related to correlational selection but should not be conflated with evolutionary prediction. Under standardized trait and relative-fitness coordinates, \(W_{AD}\) may correspond to a correlational selection term. Predicting phenotypic covariance, genetic correlation, trajectories, or equilibria additionally requires genetic architecture, constraints, inheritance, and dynamics. Positive local curvature can favour combinations locally without guaranteeing positive population-level covariance.

The current literature establishes the plausibility of the component routes but not the complete focal mixed partial. Floral signals can recruit antagonists, resistance can impose pollination costs, nectar chemistry can either deter or benefit pollinators, and mutualists and antagonists can have non-additive fitness effects. These findings motivate the model, but heterogeneous studies across traits and taxa cannot be pooled into one system-specific \(D\) axis or used to calibrate the regime map.

A decisive empirical test would use a factorial manipulation of one attraction trait and one defence trait under replicated pollinator-service and antagonist-pressure contexts. Mutualist contribution, antagonist loss, direct cost, and total fitness should be measured separately. Such a design would estimate both the total interaction and the channel-specific curvatures needed to distinguish antagonist relief from mutualist interference.

## 5. Conclusions

Floral attraction and defence are locally complementary when antagonist relief exceeds pollinator interference and joint-cost curvature, and locally substitutable when the opposing contributions dominate. The value of this criterion lies less in naming a mixed partial than in making its mechanism, orientation assumptions, environmental derivatives, and inference limits explicit. The framework converts a broad verbal claim of context dependence into a set of measurable local conditions and experimental requirements.
