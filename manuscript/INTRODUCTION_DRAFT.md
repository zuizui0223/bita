# Introduction

Animal-pollinated flowers function simultaneously as signals, rewards, and mechanical interfaces through which plants obtain mutualistic services. Yet flowers are also exposed to florivores, herbivores, nectar robbers, seed predators, pathogens, and other organisms that can reduce reproductive success. Floral colour, scent, size, display, reward, and morphology may therefore affect several interaction partners at once, and their fitness consequences cannot be understood solely from pollinator attraction. Pollinator-mediated selection on individual floral traits and on combinations of traits is well established, as are the multivariate-selection concepts used to describe selection on correlated characters (Lande & Arnold 1983; Fenster et al. 2004; Harder & Johnson 2009; Schiestl & Johnson 2013). However, floral integration or phenotypic covariance may also arise from developmental covariance, shared genetic architecture, or phylogenetic constraint. Observed trait correlations therefore do not, by themselves, demonstrate adaptive correlational selection or identify the ecological mechanism producing a particular trait combination (Armbruster et al. 2014).

Non-pollinating organisms can also impose strong selection on floral phenotypes. Florivory removes reproductive tissue directly, but it can additionally modify floral display, scent, reward, and morphology, thereby altering pollinator visitation, pollen transfer, and plant reproduction (Krupnick et al. 1999; Mothershead & Marquis 2000; McCall & Irwin 2006). Signals that recruit mutualists may also be exploited by antagonists. In *Cucurbita pepo*, experimentally enhanced floral fragrance increased attraction of florivorous beetles and reduced reproduction, demonstrating a direct ecological cost of advertising (Theis & Adler 2012). Such evidence rules out the general assumption that stronger attraction only increases pollination benefits. It does not, however, imply that antagonists invariably weaken selection for attraction, because the outcome may vary with antagonist identity, density, phenology, and community composition.

Flower-specific defence and access-limitation traits are similarly multifunctional. Chemical defences, trichomes, sticky surfaces, narrow floral tubes, mechanical barriers, and secondary metabolites in nectar may deter florivores or exploiters while also altering legitimate pollinator access, reward consumption, learning, residence time, and pollen transfer. An ecological cost of resistance expressed through pollination was demonstrated in *Brassica rapa*, where herbivore-resistant lines differed in floral traits and pollinator use (Strauss et al. 1999). Nevertheless, defence-associated traits do not have uniformly negative effects on pollinators. Nectar secondary metabolites can deter foragers at some concentrations, enhance learning or memory at others, and reduce pathogen loads in pollinators (Wright et al. 2013; Richardson et al. 2015; Stevenson et al. 2017). Thus, a trait labelled as “defence” cannot generally be treated as a single allocation cost, nor can pollinator interference be inferred from that label alone.

The fitness effects of mutualists and antagonists may also be non-additive. A factorial field experiment manipulating pollination and herbivory showed that their joint effects on plant fitness were not a simple sum and suggested a pathway to correlated evolution of traits involved in mutualistic and antagonistic interactions (Herrera et al. 2002). Likewise, flower-dwelling crab spiders may deter pollinators while indirectly altering floral-signal evolution by removing florivores (Knauer et al. 2018). These studies establish that the net consequences of attraction, defence, and antagonist suppression can depend on the combination of ecological interactions. They therefore make a fixed attraction–defence trade-off implausible as a universal expectation. At the same time, they do not provide a general rule that attraction and defence should instead be positively associated.

The unresolved problem is not whether pollinators and antagonists both matter, but how their known effects combine for one explicitly defined attraction trait and one explicitly defined flower-specific defence or barrier trait. Let \(A\) denote a focal attraction trait, \(D\) a focal defence or access-limitation trait, and \(W\) a declared fitness or biological-outcome scale. The local mixed partial

\[
W_{AD}=\frac{\partial^2 W}{\partial A\,\partial D}
\]

measures how the marginal effect of one trait changes with the other. Positive \(W_{AD}\) indicates local complementarity on the declared scale, whereas negative \(W_{AD}\) indicates local substitutability. This quantity is closely related to cross-trait fitness curvature and, under appropriate trait and relative-fitness scaling, to correlational selection (Lande & Arnold 1983; Phillips & Arnold 1989; Blows & Brooks 2003). It is therefore not a new mathematical object. More importantly, it does not by itself imply phenotypic covariance, genetic correlation, an evolutionary trajectory, an optimum, or an evolutionarily stable strategy.

A second inferential problem is that the same total fitness curvature can arise from different biological mechanisms. A defence trait may increase the marginal value of attraction by reducing the additional antagonist damage generated by stronger attraction. The same defence trait may instead reduce the marginal value of attraction by obstructing legitimate pollinators. Attraction and defence may also share direct construction, allocation, or physiological costs. Accordingly, the local attraction–defence interaction can be represented conceptually as

\[
W_{AD}
=
\text{antagonist relief}
-
\text{mutualist interference}
-
\text{joint-cost curvature}.
\]

This expression is a bookkeeping identity rather than, by itself, a biological explanation. Total \(W_{AD}\) cannot uniquely identify the three component mechanisms without channel-specific measurements or manipulations. Likewise, evidence that a defence trait reduces pollinator visitation establishes a possible defence-to-pollinator route, but it does not show that the trait changes the marginal pollination return to the same focal attraction trait. Evidence that attraction recruits antagonists is similarly insufficient to estimate antagonist-relief curvature.

Here, we develop a focal-trait local diagnostic framework for one declared attraction trait, one declared flower-specific defence or barrier trait, and one declared outcome scale. We first separate the signed decomposition of total curvature from the additional orientation assumptions required to interpret the component terms as non-negative mechanism magnitudes. We then derive the local sign condition under which antagonist relief exceeds pollinator interference and joint-cost curvature, producing complementarity rather than substitutability. Finally, we derive the additional local derivative conditions required for changes in pollinator service or antagonist pressure to shift this balance. The framework is intentionally narrower than a full evolutionary model: it diagnoses local marginal-fitness interactions and their mechanistic interpretation, while keeping those results separate from claims about trait covariance, genetic architecture, adaptive dynamics, or evolutionary endpoints. We ask: (1) under what local mechanism balance are floral attraction and defence complementary versus substitutable; (2) under what conditions do ecological changes move that balance; and (3) which channel-specific measurements are required to identify the mechanism behind an observed total interaction?
