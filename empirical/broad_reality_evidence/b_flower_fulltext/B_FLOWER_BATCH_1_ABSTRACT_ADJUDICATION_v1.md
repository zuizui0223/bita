# B-flower Batch 1: extract-first abstract adjudication v1

## Scope

This file records the **18 unique works** from the 56-record B-flower Batch 1
whose titles and available abstracts explicitly suggest a flower-organ barrier
or deterrent, an animal response, and a potentially recoverable study design.

It is a reading-order decision only.

```text
56 Batch 1 candidates
  ├─ 18 extract-first abstract candidates (this file and CSV)
  ├─ 18 screen-second candidates
  └─ 20 quick scope-check candidates
```

The latter 38 records are deliberately not called excluded. B-to-antagonist was
censused precisely because sparse evidence must not be silently discarded.

## What extract-first means

An extract-first record has abstract-level signals for all of:

1. flower, nectar, inflorescence, petal, pollen, or reproductive-structure
   mechanism plausibly assignable to `B_flower`;
2. pollinator, nectar-thief, floral-antagonist, florivory, damage, or attack
   response;
3. an experiment, choice assay, or trait--response association that might allow
   a compatible effect size.

It does **not** mean that the record is included, causal, independent, or
numerically extractable.

## Full-text gate

For every extract-first work, record the following before an effect can enter a
meta-analysis:

```text
trait is flower-organ matched B_flower
animal response is pollinator or floral antagonist
study design is declared
outcome family is declared
comparison or association is recoverable
variance or sufficient statistics are recoverable
independent study cluster is identifiable
```

The first effect-size checks should prioritize nectar secondary metabolites
(alkaloids, nicotine, cardenolides, non-protein amino acids), floral volatile
studies with an explicit defensive function, and physical barriers such as
slippery flowers or secretory trichomes. These are not pooled together; they
remain separate route × design × outcome × effect-family cells.

## Deliberate caution

Several extract-first works may ultimately move to A rather than B, or become
mechanistic cases rather than pooled effects. For example, a floral volatile
can be an attraction signal, a deterrent, or both; full-text manipulation and
animal-response evidence determines its final role.
