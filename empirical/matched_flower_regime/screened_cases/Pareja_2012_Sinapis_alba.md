## What the study directly establishes

The study experimentally infested vegetative tissues with aphids and measured
floral volatile emission. Specialist *Lipaphis erysimi* reduced floral volatile
production relative to the other treatments; a later field assay recorded total
insect landings on flowers during four one-hour observation periods.

The study reported no difference in total flower visits among uninfested,
*L. erysimi*-infested, and *Myzus persicae*-infested plants:

```text
F(2, 35) = 0.02; P = 0.985
UD: 14.5 ± 3.1 visits per day
Le: 13.9 ± 3.1 visits per day
Mp: 14.5 ± 3.1 visits per day
```

## Why no `A_to_pollination` record is registered

Floral volatile emission is a plausible `A_flower` trait, and pollinator
visitation is a permitted pollination outcome. However, the reported visitor
analysis compares **aphid-infestation treatments**, not directly manipulated or
modelled floral volatile emission. Aphid infestation changes whole-plant
condition and vegetative volatile cues in addition to floral scent. The study
therefore does not identify the direct trait-to-pollination path required by the
four-path registry.

It also does not measure a flower-stage antagonist response. The manipulated
antagonist feeds on vegetative phloem, so the paper cannot supply either floral
antagonism arrow without an explicit cross-organ bridge.

## Reusable insight, outside the active registry

The paper is useful background for a separate cross-organ hypothesis: vegetative
herbivory can alter floral scent while not necessarily changing total flower
visitation in the tested field context. That proposition is not equivalent to a
direct `A_flower → pollination` effect and is intentionally kept outside the
active floral A×D synthesis.
