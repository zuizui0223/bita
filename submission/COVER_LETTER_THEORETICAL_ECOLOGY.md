# Cover letter draft — Theoretical Ecology

Dear Editors,

Please consider our manuscript, **“When are floral attraction and defence complementary? A local mechanistic diagnostic under mutualists, antagonists, and joint costs,”** for publication as a Research Article in *Theoretical Ecology*.

Animal-pollinated flowers must attract mutualists while remaining exposed to florivores, nectar robbers, pathogens, and other antagonists. Existing work has established that floral signals can recruit both mutualists and antagonists, that defence can carry pollination costs, and that pollinators and herbivores may have non-additive effects on plant fitness. What remains difficult is to infer, for one explicitly defined attraction trait and one flower-specific defence or access-limitation trait, whether the defence increases or decreases the local marginal return to attraction and which biological pathway produces that interaction.

We develop a focal-trait local framework around the mixed partial of a declared fitness or biological-outcome surface. The contribution is not a new type of mixed partial or correlational selection. Instead, the framework separates antagonist relief, interference with legitimate pollinators, and direct joint-cost curvature; states the orientation assumptions required before these terms can be interpreted as non-negative mechanism magnitudes; and shows why total fitness curvature alone cannot identify the component mechanisms. We further derive the unrestricted environmental derivative balance and distinguish it from separable and linear special cases. This prevents verbal claims such as “more antagonists favour complementarity” from being treated as universal predictions.

A reproducible endpoint-normalized sensitivity analysis illustrates that both complementary and substitutable local interactions occur within the declared model family. Across 2,592 evaluations, the sign changes with biological route strengths and response shape. These counts are reported strictly as finite-design diagnostics, not as empirical frequencies. A preliminary literature layer is used only to establish biological plausibility of selected pathways and is not presented as a systematic review, parameter calibration, or empirical validation of the regime map.

We believe the manuscript fits *Theoretical Ecology* because it uses an explicit theoretical framework to address an ecological question at the intersection of plant–pollinator mutualism, floral antagonism, and trait-mediated interaction modification. The framework is designed to remain interpretable to empirical ecologists and concludes with the factorial measurements needed to identify each mechanism in real systems.
